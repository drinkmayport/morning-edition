Craft beer: three straight years of decline

The BA's annual report confirms craft production fell 5.1% in 2025, with 481 breweries closing and total U.S. craft brewery count dropping to 9,578 — the third consecutive year of declines. However, craft's volume share of total beer ticked up slightly to 13.3%, and the BA economist sees 2026 as a potential 'buying the dip' moment for well-positioned entrants.

⚡RELEVANT TO YOU — Survival intel for Mayport: 60% of breweries reported production declines, but focused, differentiated breweries are holding steady — know which bucket you're in heading into summer.

THC drinks boom despite looming November ban

Delta-9 THC drink sales surged 148% YoY in the 52 weeks ending February 22 per SPINS, even as a Congressionally-mandated November deadline threatens the category's legal existence. Brands are doubling down on distribution through the three-tier beer system while hedging with hemp-free innovation — a strategic playbook any craft brewery considering a THC line should study closely right now.

⚡RELEVANT TO YOU — Florida is a key THC beverage battleground; the three-tier distribution path these brands are using runs directly through beer distributors — Mayport should be in active conversations now about positioning before the November regulatory cliff.

RTDs hit $13.6B; spirits-based up 25.7%

Spirits-based RTDs grew 25.7% in 2025, offsetting roughly $650 million in declines in core spirits, as the total RTD segment now commands 12.7% of off-premise bev-alc dollars. Distributors are rapidly reorganizing their portfolios around RTD velocity — any craft brewery not actively exploring a canned cocktail or FMB extension is ceding shelf real estate and wholesaler mindshare.

⚡RELEVANT TO YOU — Florida's convenience store and beach-market RTD velocity is among the highest in the country — a well-positioned Mayport canned cocktail or hard seltzer could be a meaningful revenue diversifier in 2026.

Foraged mixed-culture beers blur wine boundaries

Dan Wye of Fyne Ales' Origins project is fermenting mixed-culture beers with foraged botanicals engineered to replicate wine aromas and textures — with no grapes — in an attempt to define an entirely new style.
